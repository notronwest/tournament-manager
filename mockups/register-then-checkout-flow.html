Mockup — Register-then-Checkout flow

Variant of the cart mockup. Registration and checkout are separate processes: clicking Register on an event creates a real event_registrations row with status='pending_payment' and holds the slot. Pending regs accumulate in a "cart" view; the player checks out when they're done browsing. Payment flips them from pending to paid (and fires partner invites).

Compare with mockups/registration-checkout-flow.html — the cart-first version where nothing was written to the DB until checkout. The two diverge mostly on capacity / visibility / partner-invite timing decisions; the player-visible screens look similar.
Defaults baked into this mockup. Each is a real decision worth pushing back on.
  • Register creates a real DB row with status='pending_payment'. Visible to organizers in attendees view (with a "pending" pill).
  • Capacity holds — silent. Pending-payment regs DO count against max_teams, but the user NEVER sees a countdown timer. A backstage cleanup job releases pending regs that have been idle for ~30 min. If something gets released while the user was away, the checkout page explains it calmly and offers re-register / waitlist.
  • Partner invites fire at checkout, not at register. Safer — partner isn't notified about a half-baked registration. (Open question below covers the alternative.)
  • Persistent bottom bar shows whenever you have pending regs anywhere on the site. Count + total + a fat Check out button. No countdown — anxiety belongs to the system, not the user.
  • Social proof — momentum, not live counts. Tournament header shows "X players registered this week" / "Filling fast — 3 spots left." Based on real historical data, no Amazon-style "8 people viewing right now."
  • Single-tournament checkout. Checking out covers all your pending regs for a single tournament. If you have pending regs across multiple tournaments, the bottom bar groups by tournament and you pay each separately. (Stripe Connect math is per-org anyway.)

Holds are silent. A backstage cleanup job runs after a quiet timeout (say, 30 minutes of inactivity) and releases any pending-payment regs that have been idle — but the user never sees a countdown timer anywhere. When something does get released, the checkout page explains what changed in calm language and offers recovery (re-register or join a waitlist where applicable). Open questions for the design conversation
  • Do pending-payment regs count against capacity? Defaulted to YES with a 30-min hold. Alternative: NO, regs are effectively cart-state until paid (matches cart-first mockup more closely). YES is more honest about slot availability; NO avoids the auto-cancel cleanup job.
  • Partner invite timing. Defaulted to AT-CHECKOUT. Alternative: AT-REGISTER, with a "Bob registered you but hasn't paid yet — your spot won't be held until he does" note in the invite email. Lets partners respond earlier but risks ghost-partnering.
  • Hold-expiry semantics. Auto-cancel after 30 min, or auto-extend if the user is active on the site? Active-extension is gentler for slow browsers but penalizes anyone who walked away from a session.
  • What's in the pending-bar when multiple tournaments are pending? Group by tournament with a per-tournament Check out, or show a unified total with a dropdown? Mockup shows the former, but it gets awkward with 3+ tournaments.
  • What about editing a pending reg? Can the user swap partners on a pending Mens 3.5 before paying? Probably yes — same edit-mode register page as today, but the partner-change side effects (cancel email, etc.) don't fire since no invite has gone out yet.
  • Stripe Connect cross-org behavior. Each tournament's checkout is a separate Stripe session because each org has its own Connect account. Fine in theory; awkward if the user has pendings across 5 tournaments and has to do 5 checkouts. Probably rare in practice, but worth flagging.
  • Free tournaments. If total is $0, skip Stripe entirely and just flip pending → paid on the Pay button. Stripe Checkout requires > $0.
Take this back to the other mockup to compare: mockups/registration-checkout-flow.html. Same player- facing happy path; the key difference is what's in the DB during the "browsing + adding" phase. Decide which property matters more — immediate organizer visibility into who's interested (register-then-checkout) vs. zero state to clean up if someone abandons (cart-first).
