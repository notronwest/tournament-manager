erne & bert — 10 logo directions

First pass. All ten lean on typography rather than illustration — the brand name is the joke and it should carry the mark. The accent colour, when used, is one of clay terracotta, kitchen-line yellow, deep court blue, or pulled-back charcoal. Read each twice: once on the light stage, once on the dark. React to shapes and weight, not finished polish.

01 — Lowercase wordmark

Tight grotesque + italic serif ampersand

Modern lowercase wordmark in Inter Tight. The italic serif ampersand (Fraunces) carries the personality and lifts the piece without leaning on an illustration. Friendly, current, works at any size.

02 — Editorial serif

Playfair caps with wide tracking

High-contrast Didone serif, deliberately set wide. Reads as "we take this seriously" — venue-program, printed draw, awards plaque. Sport with a sense of occasion.

e&b stacked over the URL

The whole name compresses to three glyphs — favicon-ready, avatar-ready, embroiderable. The lowercase mono URL beneath works as a footer credit on a t-shirt; drop it on web / app marks.

05 — Stacked block

DM Serif Display, three-line cube

Brutalist three-line block. Same visual width on top and bottom rows, italic ampersand sandwiched between. Works as an avatar, a stamp, embroidery on a chest pocket.

06 — Hand-script

Caveat with a paddle-swing underline

Reads "weekly group chat," not "venture brand." The underline lifts at the end like a paddle finish. Risky — feels casual for handling money. Worth seeing.

Roboto Slab, venue-banner weight

Hairline-stroked, no fill

Quiet, considered, gallery wall. The italic Fraunces ampersand stays filled so the eye has somewhere to rest. Pairs well with photography — sits over a court shot without competing.

09 — Two-tone team

Clay + court-blue split

Each name in its own colour, joined by a muted ampersand. Quiet nod to doubles — two players, one team. Risk: every other team-colour treatment in sport looks like a sports brand. Lean further into the typography if we keep it.

10 — Mark + wordmark

Two dots in a circle, Inter Tight wordmark

The mark is two dots inside a ring — two players on a court — abstract enough to read as a logo first, a metaphor second. Works as a standalone favicon; pairs with the wordmark on the navbar.
